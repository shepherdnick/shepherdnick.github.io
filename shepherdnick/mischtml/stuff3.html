Steel

Steel is the name given to a large family of metals called alloys.  They can contain different amounts of iron and carbon and other elements.

The composition of steel is determined by its job.  Iron containing 4% carbon is extremely brittle, but with 0.1% however, steel is easily drawn into wire.  1% carbon content steel is stronger without being too brittle and makes huge cables, which support bridges like the Humber Bridge.

Alloys

An alloy is a mixture of a metal with one or more other elements.  The components are mixed together while molten and allowed to cool to form a uniform solid.  The presence of other elements in a metal changes its properties and can often increase its strength.  For example, brass is an alloy of copper, and zinc and is stronger than either pure metal.  Duralumin is used for building aircraft: it is an alloy of aluminium and copper with smaller amounts of magnesium, iron and silicon.

Brittle steel is a problem for steel makers and this can be caused by the impurities caused by oxygen, nitrogen, phosphorus and sulphur.  These types of steel cannot be used for something like gas pipelines under water, or oil rigs.

Changing the content of the steel is not the only way that it’s properties can be altered.  The amount that the steel is let to cool and heat again can change the properties – and this procedure is called heat treatment.  Working the steel, and hammering it is called work treatment.

Steel is a very versatile material because its properties can be changed in order to meet properties of its use.  Certain properties are often called Specification Steels.

How is steel made?

Most steel consists of impure iron from a blast furnace, produced as molten liquid.  The iron has many other elements dissolved in it.  The most important are phosphorus, carbon, manganese, sulphur and silicon.

This type of metal is very brittle and to turn it into steel, the carbon content must be lowered.  Most of the phosphorus and sulphur are removed, and other elements added before the material is allowed to solidify.

All of this is achieved in the Basic Oxygen Steel making (BOS) Process, in which batches of about 300 tonnes of high quality steel are made in just 40 minutes.  Making steel of high quality is a highly skilled process.

Removing unwanted elements

About 300 tonnes of molten iron from a blast furnace are poured into a huge container called a ladle.

Sulphur is the first element to be removed.  This is done in a separate process before the steel making process begins.  Through a lance, several kilograms of powdered magnesium is injected into the ladle producing a violent exothermic reaction where sulphur is reduced to magnesium sulphide, of which floats to the surface and is raked off.

Mg + S à MgS

The remaining desulphurised molten iron is poured into a converter containing scrap steel.  A water-cooled lance is injected to the surface of the molten iron, and a supersonic jet of oxygen under pressure forces its way into the vessel and creates a seething foam of molten metal and gas, which is blasted up the walls of the converter.  Over the next 20 minutes or so, most of the impurities, such as carbon, silicon, manganese and phosphorus, as well as some of the iron are oxidised.

The oxides of phosphorus and silicon are acidic and will react with bases to form salts.  Soon after the oxygen blow has started, a mixture of calcium oxide and magnesium oxide (made by heating limestone and dolomite) is added to the converter.

These are basic (B from BOS) oxides and react with acidic oxides to form a molten slag, which floats to the surface and can be scooped off (also containing the oxides of manganese and iron).  The slag can be separated because they have different densities and form two layers.

Keeping Track

Computers in a control room monitor the whole steel making process.  2 minutes before the end of the oxygen blow, a sub-lance is injected into the converter to take a sample of the molten iron to test the temperature and carbon content.

The computer can then work out exactly how much more oxygen should be added to reach the target composition and temperature.

Measuring the composition

Analytical chemists quickly measure the sample using atomic emission spectroscopy.  This involves making the steel sample into an electrode for an electric arc, so that each element present emits a characteristic line spectrum.  The intensities of the lines correspond to the concentration of atoms of each element.  At this stage the analysis involves only a few elements, but the method can be used to measure up to 20 elements.

During the oxygen blow, the elements are oxidised in a sequence.  When most of the impurities have been removed, some of the iron is also oxidised.  This is unavoidable but kept to a minimum.

Controlling the temperature

Careful control of the temperature is a vital part of making high quality steel.  At the end of the oxygen blow, a temperature of about 1700 °C – 1740 °C must be reached.  This is the target tapping temperature.  Higher temperatures waste expensive energy and can cause damage to the converter linings.  Lower temperatures can be even more costly is the metal solidifies before it is supposed to.

In the converter, none of the process is externally heated; all the heat is provided by the violent exothermic reactions from the oxygen.  Some of the energy is lost to the surroundings, but most is used to heat the converter.  The scrap steel acts as a coolant, and a source of recycled steel.

At the end of the blow

At the end of the blow, the converter is rotated to pour off the molten steel through a tap in the side of the vessel.  It is then tilted in the opposite direction to drain off the slag.

Lumps of aluminium are added to the molten steel to act as an agent, which absorbs any excess oxygen that has been dissolved in the metal, forming aluminium oxide, which floats to the surface.

Meeting the specification

By now the elements which are not wanted and also some that are have been removed from the iron.  The elements, which are wanted, have to be put back.  The computer model predicts the exact quantity of each substance needed to achieve the specification for the particular batch.

Carbon, manganese and silicon, which were removed in the blow, are often added at this stage.  Chromium and more aluminium are also common additions.  More exotic elements such as niobium, molybdenum and tungsten may also be added.  Argon is blown into the steel (as it is a gas) by using a lance (much like in the oxygen blow process) – to make sure the mixture stirs and the composition and temperature are uniform.

Now, several processes are available whilst the molten steel is in the ladle.  The ladle arc furnace allows steel in the ladles to be reheated electrically so that it is possible to control the temperature precisely.  The ladle arc furnace is particularly important in producing high C and low P steels which are used to make steel rods.

Throughout the addition process, the temperature is closely monitored and once again, samples are taken and analysed.  This information is used to constantly update the computer predictions.

A final ‘trimming’ adjusts the concentrations of elements in the steel to the required values and the molten steel is now ready for casting.  This is usually a continuous process, by casting very long strands of solid metal or it may be poured into moulds in which it solidifies into ingots, rather like making ice cubes.

Can steel making be improved?

The production of steel continues to rise around the world, even though plastics are taking over some of the processes of steel.  This is due to the fact that steel making has become a very exact science, and there is a growing demand for specific steels with consistent compositions.

The BOS process is constantly being reviewed, so that any new equipment can be installed to make the steel at a higher quality.  Some of the other ideas include ways to save energy and to be more efficient in the steel production.

Steel is becoming more and more recycled and the electronic arc furnace is being more widely used for these types of steel.

Electronic Arc Furnace

An electronic arc furnace uses old scrap steel, which is melted by the heat generated when a spark is produced between carbon electrodes.  Lime is added and the impurities removed as slag.

By carefully selecting the scrap and making necessary additions, relatively small batches of steel are made to meet given specifications.

Rusting

A return to nature

Many materials including iron occur in the Earth’s crust as oxides.  This is because the change from a metal to an oxide is a more energetically favourable process.  The oxide is more stable than the metal.  To extract the metal from its ore requires a lot of energy – and this is why iron tends to re-form back to its ore – in the form of rusting.  Rusting is the common name for the corrosion of iron.

The rusting of steel is a familiar problem to car owners – the iron returns to nature.  Cars rust because the steel they are made from reacts with oxygen and water in the atmosphere.  When iron or steel rusts a hydrated form of iron(III) oxide with variable composition (Fe2O3.xH2O) is produced.  The oxide is permeable to air and water and does not form a protective layer on the metal surface, so the metal continues to corrode under the layers of rust.

Iron and steel will rust whenever they are in contact with moist air, but the rate of rusting is greatly influenced by other factors such as impurities in the iron, the presence of acids or other electrolytes in the solution in contact with the iron, and the availability of oxygen dissolved in the solution.

What happens during rusting?

Rusting is an electrochemical process.  Electrochemical cells are set up in the metal surface, where different areas act as sites of oxidation and reduction.
